Disabilities in the United States

The 2021 American Community Survey by the Census Bureau reported 13% of all Americans have a non-institutionalized disability. Of the roughly 38,903,887 adults with a disability in the United States, 19.7% of them live below the poverty line. This is almost double the number of adults in the United States who are below the poverty line. This is partially due to lower rates of employment for disabled people. In the United States, 25.9% of disabled people over the age of 16 are employed in the civilian sector, while 59.6% of all US citizens over 16 are employed in the same sector.

Comparing the breakdown of disability between my current state of residence, Utah, and the United States, Utah has a disabled population of 342,378 or 10.3% of the total population, compared to the United States which has 42,485,034 disabled people or 13.0% of the total population.

Table 1 - Summary:
This table shows the disparities between genders (male, female) and types of disabilities. The 7 rows represent six disability types: cognitive, hearing, mobility, vision, self-care, and independent living, as well as the percentage of individuals reporting no disability. The columns show the corresponding population percentages by gender.

Key highlights:

  • Cognitive disabilities are more common among females than males in both Utah and the U.S.
  • Hearing disabilities are more prevalent in males across both regions.
  • Mobility and vision disabilities tend to be higher nationally than in Utah.
  • Independent living disabilities show a higher rate among females nationally.
  • The majority of people in both Utah and the U.S. report no disability, with slightly higher rates in Utah.

There are a few reasons why these disparities exist; however, that is not a topic for this page.

When it comes to representation by race, there are some interesting differences between Utah and the United States. Table 2: Disability Percent by Race, you can see that Utah has a higher occurrence of disabilities in the Black or African American, American Indian and Alaska Natives, Native Hawai’ian and Other Pacific Islanders, and the Hispanic or Latino communities than the United States as a whole. However, for the White community in Utah, they have a lower occurrence of disability than the national rate.

When we take into consideration the rates of disabilities in the United States (roughly 40 million people) and Utah (roughly 350,000 people), we can understand the importance of building accessible experiences for all people.

Universal Design is Design for All

Even the creator of the web, Tim Berners-Lee understood the importance of inclusion in web design:

The power of the web is in its universality. Access by everyone regardless is an essential aspect.

This website applies the principles of Universal Design, as defined by Ronald Mace and his team:

  • Equitable Use
    The design is useful and marketable to people with diverse abilities
  • Flexibility in Use
    The design accommodates a wide range of individual preferences and abilities
  • Simple and Intuitive Use
    Use of the design is easy to understand, regardless of the user’s experience, knowledge, language skills, or current concentration level
  • Perceptible Information
    The design communicates necessary information effectively to the user, regardless of ambient conditions or the user’s sensory abilities
  • Tolerance for Error
    The design minimizes hazards and the adverse consequences of accidental or unintended actions
  • Low Physical Effort
    The design can be used efficiently and comfortably and with a minimum of fatigue
  • Size and Space for Approach and Use
    Appropriate size and space is provided for approach, reach, manipulation, and use regardless of user’s body size, posture, or mobility

It has also been built to meet the Level AA requirements of WCAG 2.1.
